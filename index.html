The Thermodynamics of Aaradhya

A late-night text about cheese proved that even in the middle of exam season, the chemistry between us is the only thing that’s truly stable.

By Niv  |  February 26, 2026

There is a specific hour in the study cycle, usually past midnight, when the brain ceases to be a catalog of facts and becomes something more porous. The hierarchy of information collapses. The periodic table holds no more weight than a grocery list. Everything is just data, floating in the exhaustion.

Two nights ago, Aaradhya reached this point. She has been studying for a chemistry exam that is now forty-eight hours away. It is an important exam, the kind that feels like a heavy door opening or closing on a future. She sent a text in the dark.

pizza
cheese
pepperoni

It was a small moment—a collision between the academic pressure she is under and the simple, human need for dinner. But reading it, I realized that I wasn't just looking at a tired student. I was looking at Aaradhya, whose brain was valiantly trying to bridge the gap between biology and comfort food.

I didn't want to reply with just words. Words feel cheap when you're staring down the barrel of organic chemistry. So, I decided to speak her language. I drew a diagram on my phone and sent it back. If we are doing chemistry, let’s do chemistry.

Figure 1. The Reaction Coordinate of Us. Note that the process is spontaneous (Gibbs Free Energy is negative) and the final state is significantly more stable than the reactants.

I wanted her to smile, but I also wanted to remind her of a fundamental truth: We are a spontaneous reaction. In chemistry, a negative Delta G means a process happens naturally, without being forced. That is how I feel about her. No matter how much stress the exam applies to the system, the equilibrium of "us" doesn't shift.

But the chemistry doesn't stop at energy profiles. As I thought about her studying those mechanisms—nucleophilic attacks and leaving groups—I realized that she is actually the central component of my entire equation. So I sketched out one more figure, just to be sure the data was peer-reviewed.

Figure 2. The Synthesis of Happiness. Aaradhya acts as both the reagent and the catalyst, lowering the activation energy required to have a perfect day.

The exam on February 28 is a snapshot. It measures what she knows about molecules on a specific Thursday morning. But these figures measure something permanent. They measure the fact that she is the most stable element in my life.

Aaradhya is going to be fine. She will walk into that exam hall, and she will write down what she knows. But even if she forgets the bond angles or the reaction rates, she has already mastered the most important chemistry there is. She is loved, she is appreciated, and—most importantly—she deserves that pizza.
